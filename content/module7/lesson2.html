Design Systems: Driving Consistency and Innovation

Companies like Airbnb, Uber, and IBM have changed the ways they design digital products by incorporating their own unique design systems. By utilizing a collection of reusable components and a set of standards guiding the use of those components, each of these companies has been able to accelerate the pace of creation and foster innovation within their teams.

While many organizations have what they consider to be a design system, these collections typically amount to no more than a group of elements and code snippets. A true design system is more than just a style guide or pattern library; it includes comprehensive standards and documentation that accompany the design assets, making the components easier to use and their purpose clearer to discern.

The Importance of Design Systems

A design system is a cohesive collection of reusable components, guided by clear standards, that can be assembled together to build any number of applications consistently. It's not merely a collection of reusable UI elements; it has an underlying structure and meaning, embodying a system of concepts and principles that govern the design.

Understanding not only the what, but the why, behind the design of a system is critical to creating an exceptional user experience. Defining and adhering to standards is how we create that understanding and ensure consistency across the product.

Design systems help teams work more efficiently, maintain consistency across products and platforms, and scale their design efforts more effectively. They also facilitate collaboration between designers and developers, as the system provides a shared language and set of guidelines for both teams to work from.

Building a Design System

Here are the key steps and benefits to building a robust design system:

  1. Conduct a visual audit: Take inventory of the CSS and visual qualities of the elements in your current design to gauge the scope and complexity of the undertaking.
  2. Create a visual design language: Establish the core components that will be used to construct your digital product, including:
    • Color (primaries, tints, and shades that represent your brand)
    • Typography (headings, body copy, and monospace for code, with a limited number of font families)
    • Sizing and spacing (using a consistent scale, such as a 4-based scale, for rhythm and balance)
    • Imagery (guidelines for illustrations, icons, and other graphics)
  3. Create a UI/pattern library: Collect all the components of your UI currently in production, such as buttons, forms, modals, and images. Merge and remove what you don't need, consolidating the essential building blocks.
  4. Document what each component is and when to use it: This step is crucial, as comprehensive documentation and clearly defined standards separate a true design system from a mere pattern library or style guide.

By following these steps and incorporating a well-designed system into their workflows, organizations can drive consistency, collaboration, and innovation within their teams, enabling them to create exceptional user experiences more efficiently and at scale.
