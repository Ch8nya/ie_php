h2>Data Dashboards: Visualizing Information Effectively

Data dashboards are essential tools for displaying critical information in a concise and easily understandable manner. They are commonly used in web applications, intranets, and business intelligence contexts to provide users with regularly updated data that they need to monitor frequently.

Definition and Purpose

A dashboard is a collection of data visualizations presented in a single-page view, designed to impart at-a-glance information that users can act upon quickly. The term "dashboard" is a metaphor derived from car dashboards, which provide essential information that drivers can absorb quickly without requiring extensive cognitive processing.

Dashboards are not intended to facilitate in-depth exploration of complex data. Instead, their primary goal is to provide users with the necessary information that can be consumed rapidly, with minimal interaction or cognitive effort. Users should be able to glance at the dashboard and immediately grasp the answers to critical questions relevant to their tasks or objectives.

Types of Data Dashboards

Data dashboards generally exist in two forms:

  • Operational Dashboards: These dashboards aim to impart critical information quickly to users engaged in time-sensitive tasks, such as monitoring server availability, patient vitals during surgery, customer service calls, or flight traffic. Operational dashboards typically present continuously updating data, allowing users to identify unacceptable deviations or visualize available resources.
  • Analytical Dashboards: Unlike operational dashboards, analytical dashboards provide users with at-a-glance information for analysis and decision-making purposes, without the same level of time sensitivity. These dashboards help users identify the need for further investigation, research, or analysis, such as monitoring fluctuating sales data that may require thoughtful examination.

Both operational and analytical dashboards require a single-screen view of the data they monitor and must effectively communicate important information to users at a glance.

Preattentive Processing and Quantitative Representation

To enhance the user's ability to quickly comprehend relationships within the data, it is beneficial to leverage the properties of human visual perception, specifically preattentive processing. Preattentive processing refers to the ability to perceive certain visual attributes without fully engaging attention, such as length, area, angle, 2D position, and color.

While all preattentive features stand out easily, not all are equally effective in representing quantitative information. For example, while we can effortlessly notice a large rectangle among many small rectangles, we are not as adept at judging the precise size difference. On the other hand, we are skilled at estimating how lengths compare and perceiving position in a 2D space, making these two preattentive attributes ideal for quantitative representation.

Choosing the Right Visualization

When selecting visualizations for data dashboards, it is crucial to consider the following principles:

  • Linear vs. Area-Based Graphs: Bar charts and line graphs that utilize length and 2D position are generally more effective and easier to understand for representing quantitative data, as they capitalize on the preattentive attributes that support quick data comparisons and inferences.
  • Avoiding Circular and Area-Based Graphs: Visualizations like pie charts, gauges, and radar charts, which rely on area and angle to communicate quantitative information, are more difficult to interpret quickly or accurately. These visualizations should be avoided for communicating complex quantitative relationships.
  • 3D Graphics: Plotting 2-variable charts in 3D may improve aesthetics but can distort the shapes and alignment of visual features, making them harder to interpret accurately. 3D visualizations should be used with caution or avoided altogether in data dashboards.
  • Using Color, Shape, and Grouping: While color and shape are preattentive cues that do not easily communicate quantitative relationships, they can be used to convey categorical information and indicate which items belong together. Spatial proximity can also be used to represent semantic grouping.

By following these principles and leveraging the strengths of human visual perception, data dashboards can effectively communicate critical information to users, enabling them to make informed decisions and take appropriate actions quickly.
